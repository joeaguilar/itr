# The case for soft fallback: why deferring errors beats blocking them

**The most powerful error-handling pattern in both UX and AI is also the most counterintuitive: don't stop at failure — keep going, collect everything that's wrong, and fix it all at once.** This pattern, which we can call "soft fallback" or deferred error collection, appears independently across compiler design, functional programming, form UX, and now AI agent architectures. It works because it respects a fundamental truth about cognition and computation alike: interrupting a process at the point of failure is almost always more expensive than completing it and then correcting. The research backing this spans decades — from Bluma Zeigarnik's 1927 psychology experiments to 2025 studies on LLM agent failure modes — and the evidence consistently favors letting the flow complete.

## Blocking errors are a forced context switch — and the cost is enormous

The cognitive science is unambiguous. Gloria Mark at UC Irvine found it takes an average of **23 minutes and 15 seconds** to fully refocus after an interruption. Rubinstein, Meyer, and Evans (2001) showed task switching reduces productivity by **up to 40%**. Even 5-second interruptions triple error rates in complex cognitive work, according to the American Psychological Association. Every blocking error in a form or an agent workflow is, in cognitive terms, a forced context switch.

The Zeigarnik effect, discovered in 1927, explains why this is so painful. When a task is interrupted, the brain maintains "task-specific tension" — a persistent cognitive thread consuming working memory. Bluma Zeigarnik's original experiments showed participants recalled interrupted tasks roughly **twice as well** as completed ones, not because interruption improves memory but because the unresolved task refuses to leave working memory. When a user hits a blocking validation error at step 3 of a 7-step form, they're now carrying two cognitive loads: the incomplete primary task and the error-correction task. Soft fallback eliminates this by letting users complete the primary task first, releasing that tension, and then entering a dedicated correction phase.

Jessica Enders, author of *Designing UX: Forms*, puts it precisely: "With inline validation, users have to repeatedly switch between two quite different mental modes: form-filling and error-correcting. This slows the user down, increases cognitive mental effort, and generally reduces user satisfaction." UX Movement's research coined useful terminology here: **completion mode** (focused on filling out fields) versus **revision mode** (focused on correcting errors). Two studies (77 and 90 participants respectively) found users made significantly more errors when forced to switch between these modes constantly. The lesson: let people finish the draft, then revise.

## Form UX already has battle-tested deferred validation patterns

The form design community has debated validation timing for over 15 years, and the research is more nuanced than the common "inline validation is always better" narrative suggests.

Luke Wroblewski's 2009 study with research firm Etre — the most-cited evidence for inline validation — showed a **22% increase in success rates**, **42% decrease in completion times**, and **47% decrease in eye fixations** for inline-validated forms. But the study had a small sample size and within-subjects design, and the specific implementation mattered enormously. Poorly implemented inline validation performed *worse* than no validation at all. The Baymard Institute, drawing on 200,000+ hours of e-commerce UX research, emphasizes three critical conditions for inline validation to work: don't validate prematurely, remove errors immediately when corrected, and use positive confirmation (green checkmarks) rather than just negative error states.

The more interesting pattern for deferred error handling is **"reward early, punish late"**, championed by Vitaly Friedman of Smashing Magazine. The idea: show positive validation immediately (confirming correct input), but defer error messages until submission or until the user has clearly finished with a field. Friedman argues this reduces distractions, avoids complex timing logic, and prevents the "change blindness" problem — where users in completion mode literally don't see error messages appearing beside fields they've already moved past.

The UK Government Digital Service (GDS) took this further with the **"Check Your Answers" pattern**, now used across British government services. Complex forms are broken into one question per page. Users complete the entire flow, then reach a summary page showing all their answers for review before final submission. The UK Parliament's design system explicitly states: "Only display error messages after a user submits the form or clicks to continue to the next step. Do not validate inputs as users are typing as it can cause stress and frustration." Adam Silver applied this pattern to Just Eat's checkout, converting a single-page form to one-thing-per-page with end-of-flow review. The result: **an additional 2 million orders per year**.

Multi-step forms with deferred review can increase conversions by **up to 300%** compared to long single-page forms, according to ConversionXL research. This isn't just about deferring errors — it's about respecting the user's cognitive flow and giving them the full picture before asking them to correct anything.

## The analytics insight: knowing WHY users leave, not just WHERE

One of the most underappreciated benefits of deferred error collection is the analytics signal it generates. Traditional funnel analytics tell you **where** users drop off ("50% abandoned at step 3") but not **why**. This is the critical gap.

Zuko Analytics identifies a key abandonment category they call **"information the user doesn't have"** — when users reach a field requiring data they can't provide at that moment (Social Security number, passport number, insurance expiry date, net assets). These users don't abandon because the form is bad; they abandon because they lack the required information right now. With blocking validation, all you see is a step-3 dropout. With deferred error collection — where users complete the entire flow and the system records which fields they couldn't fill — you see something far more actionable: "This user could complete everything except their SSN and insurance policy number."

This transforms analytics from positional ("dropped at step 3") to causal ("couldn't provide SSN"). It reveals what we might call **bailout reasons** — the specific pieces of information or requirements that cause abandonment. Zuko's database revealed that users who abandoned e-commerce checkout spent **an average of 20 seconds on the promo code field**, compared to 3.5 seconds for completers — evidence that giving users a promo code field incentivizes them to leave the site and search for codes elsewhere. Field-level abandonment tracking showed email fields have a **6.4% abandonment rate** while phone number fields have **6.3%** — specific, actionable data that only emerges when you track interaction at the field level rather than the page level.

The practical implication: if you let users attempt the entire flow and record what they couldn't complete, you build a dataset of bailout reasons that directly informs product decisions. Maybe you discover 30% of abandoning users lacked their insurance policy number — so you add a "look this up later" option. You'd never learn this from standard funnel analytics.

## AI agents fail 50% of the time, and hard errors make it worse

The AI agent domain presents an even more compelling case for soft fallback. Current agent systems have roughly a **50% task completion rate** across frameworks, according to Lu et al.'s 2025 evaluation of TaskWeaver, MetaGPT, and AutoGen. Augment Code reports that **41-86.7% of multi-agent LLM systems fail in production**, with most breakdowns occurring within hours of deployment. Stanford's 2023 AI Index Report found that **67% of AI system failures in production stem from improper error handling** rather than core algorithmic issues. The error handling pattern itself is the dominant failure mode.

The default behavior in most frameworks is catastrophically simple: **hard-fail on unhandled exceptions**. LangChain stops agent execution entirely on an unhandled tool error unless you explicitly set `handle_tool_error=True`. This means a single API timeout in a 20-step workflow throws away all previous computation. The agent loses its accumulated context, reasoning chain, and intermediate results.

Error cascading compounds the problem. Galileo's analysis illustrates this vividly: "Hallucinated facts don't stay contained; they become inputs for subsequent decisions. That phantom SKU doesn't just create one bad database entry. It corrupts pricing logic at step 6, triggers inventory checks at step 9, generates shipping labels at step 12, and sends customer confirmations at step 15." When an agent hits one error and attempts to retry or backtrack, it often generates new errors from the recovery attempt itself, creating a cascade that consumes the context window.

## Context windows are scarce, and retries waste them

The token economics of hard-fail-and-retry are brutal. **Agents make 5-20x more LLM calls per task** than single-pass applications due to iterative planning, tool selection, result evaluation, and error recovery loops. Every retry adds the error message, the recovery attempt, and new response tokens to the context window. One documented case showed an agent consuming approximately **35,600 tokens per message** in complex workspaces, with **93.5% of tokens wasted** on redundant re-injection of workspace files.

Even with large context windows, the problem doesn't go away — it transforms. Research on "context rot" from Chroma (Hong et al., 2025) measured 18 LLMs and found that models' performance grows increasingly unreliable as input length grows. A Databricks study showed model correctness begins declining around **32K tokens** for Llama 3.1 405b. When Gemini 2.5 Pro (with its 1M+ token context) was used in agentic setups, it showed "a tendency toward favoring repeating actions from its vast history rather than synthesizing novel plans" as context grew beyond ~100K tokens. More context doesn't mean better context — and retries fill that window with error noise.

A soft fallback pattern for agents would work differently: when a tool call fails or returns an unexpected result, the agent **flags the failure, records what went wrong, continues with the remaining tasks**, and then returns to address all failures at once with full knowledge of what succeeded and what didn't. Atla AI's actor-critic research validates this approach — they found that step-level error identification enabled **nearly 30 percentage point improvements** in task completion, with no re-planning or re-prompting required. The key was seeing all errors at once and applying targeted corrections rather than re-running entire workflows.

## The pattern has deep roots in computer science and programming

Soft fallback isn't new — it's a rediscovery of patterns that have proven their value across multiple domains.

**Compiler error recovery** is perhaps the oldest implementation. Early compilers stopped at the first syntax error, forcing programmers through agonizing fix-one-recompile cycles. Modern compilers use error recovery strategies (panic mode, phrase-level recovery, error productions) to continue parsing after errors and report all diagnostics at once. The Hylo compiler research (TU Delft, 2025) inserts specialized dummy nodes at error locations, carrying diagnostic information, so parsing continues uninterrupted. The CPCT+ algorithm for LR parsers **more than halved** spurious cascading errors compared to panic mode by using the complete set of minimum-cost repair sequences. The Rust compiler buffers multiple errors and emits them in order, and even runs type checking on partially-errored code. A compiler that stops at the first error is like a form that won't let you past field 1 until it's valid.

**Applicative validation** in functional programming formalizes this mathematically. The distinction between `Either` (fail-fast) and `Validated` (error-accumulating) in libraries like Scala's Cats captures the exact difference between blocking errors and soft fallback. `Either` is a Monad — it sequences dependent computations, so it *must* stop at the first failure because step 2 needs step 1's result. `Validated` is an Applicative Functor — it combines *independent* computations, so all can be evaluated regardless of individual failures. When you validate a registration form with `Validated`, you get back all errors at once (password too short, age invalid, email malformed) rather than one at a time. The Cats documentation explicitly states: "An error-accumulating data type such as Validated can't have a valid Monad instance." The mathematical structure itself encodes the soft fallback philosophy.

**Railway-oriented programming**, coined by Scott Wlaschin for F#, visualizes this as a two-track railway: a success track and a failure track. Standard ROP with `bind` is fail-fast (like `Either`), but Wlaschin's parallel composition operator `&&&` enables error accumulation for independent validations. The pattern has been implemented in F# (Chessie), JavaScript (Baccano), Kotlin, Ruby, Python, and Java.

**Optimistic UI** is the front-end cousin. Instead of blocking the user with a loading spinner (the temporal equivalent of a blocking error), the interface assumes success and immediately reflects the expected outcome. If the server later rejects the action, the UI rolls back. Instagram likes, chat message sending, and Apollo GraphQL's `optimisticResponse` all use this. The shared philosophy: let the user continue; handle problems afterward.

## Real-world analogies that make this click

The strongest analogies for a broad audience draw on familiar experiences where deferred error handling feels natural:

**TurboTax** lets you fill out your entire tax return — income, deductions, credits — and then highlights all issues at the end: missing W-2s, invalid deductions, unsigned sections. It never blocks you at "Line 7" and prevents you from continuing. This is error accumulation applied to one of the most complex forms most people encounter.

**Spell check** never blocks your typing. Red underlines run alongside your writing on a parallel "failure track," and you address all errors in a batch review pass whenever you choose. A spell checker that stopped you at every misspelled word would be unusable.

**A teacher grading an essay** reads the entire paper, marks all errors with a red pen, and returns comprehensive feedback. They don't stop reading at the first grammatical error and hand it back with "fix this and resubmit." The student gets the full picture and can address everything in one revision pass.

**A restaurant waiter** takes the entire table's order, then returns once: "We're out of the salmon, and we can't do the modification on the pasta." They don't stop after each person's order to check the kitchen.

**Medical intake forms** say "fill out what you can, leave blank what you don't know." The receptionist reviews the complete form and flags all missing fields at once. The form doesn't refuse to accept page 2 because you left a field blank on page 1.

Each of these works because the "flow" is preserved — the primary task reaches completion, and corrections happen as a focused secondary phase.

## No framework treats this as a first-class pattern yet

Despite the evidence, **no major AI agent framework implements deferred error collection as a first-class pattern**. LangGraph comes closest with state-driven error tracking and graph-encoded failure edges, allowing a node to branch to an error edge and trigger compensating actions. CrewAI has task-level error boundaries where a manager can reassign failed tasks. Microsoft AutoGen uses conversational retries where an agent reflects on its mistake and tries again. But none of them implement the full "collect all errors, continue the workflow, present a complete failure report, then correct" paradigm.

The closest named patterns from existing literature include graceful degradation (continue operating at reduced capacity), fail-soft (from hardware fault tolerance), best-effort processing (common in batch/data pipelines), and the circuit breaker pattern (from Michael Nygard's *Release It!*). But none of these precisely capture the user-facing concept of completing a task flow and then reviewing all errors together. The SHIELDA framework (2025, arxiv) identifies that existing agent exception handling is "phase-disjoint" (failing to trace symptoms to root causes) and "non-composable" (lacking structured recovery paths). It proposes a triadic model of Local Handling, Flow Control, and State Recovery — moving in the direction of soft fallback but not fully articulating it.

The term "soft fallback" fills a genuine naming gap. It bridges the UX concept (deferred validation, "Check Your Answers") with the systems concept (graceful degradation, error accumulation) and the FP concept (Applicative Validation, Validated). It's a pattern that's been independently discovered in every domain that deals with errors — and giving it a name makes it available as a deliberate design choice rather than an accidental rediscovery.

## Conclusion

The research converges on a clear thesis: **interrupting a process at the point of failure is nearly always more expensive than completing the process and correcting afterward**. The cognitive science shows context switches cost 23+ minutes to recover from and reduce productivity by 40%. Form UX research shows deferred validation preserves flow state and can generate 300% conversion improvements. AI agent studies show 50% task failure rates dominated by error cascading and context waste from retries. Compiler design proved decades ago that collecting all errors in one pass is strictly superior to stopping at the first one.

The key insight for a blog audience: soft fallback isn't about ignoring errors — it's about *when* you address them. A blocking error says "stop everything and fix this now." A soft fallback says "noted — let's finish what we're doing, and then I'll show you everything that needs attention." The former optimizes for preventing any single error; the latter optimizes for completing the overall task. In almost every domain — forms, agents, compilers, tax filing, essay grading — the latter wins. The most actionable takeaway is that this pattern also generates superior analytics: knowing that users abandon because they lack their SSN (a bailout reason) is infinitely more useful than knowing they abandoned at step 3 (a positional fact). For AI agents, seeing all five failures at once enables targeted correction rather than expensive sequential retries that pollute the context window. Soft fallback turns errors from workflow-stopping events into data — and data is always more useful than a dead end.
